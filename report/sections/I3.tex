\subsection{I.3 Improving on the Baseline}

\paragraph{Motivation and hypothesis.}
The baseline uses group size $K=2$ with Tunix's standardised group advantage
$\hat A_i = (r_i - \bar r)/\sigma_r$, where $\bar r$ and $\sigma_r$ are the mean and
standard deviation of the $K$ rewards for a prompt (see I.2). With only two samples per
group, $\sigma_r$ is estimated from two points and the group is \emph{degenerate} whenever
both completions receive equal reward (both fail the format check early, or both succeed):
then $\sigma_r=0$, the advantage is ill-defined and contributes no gradient. Even for
non-degenerate groups the two-sample standardisation is high-variance, so the effective
sample size per update is tiny. The principled modification is to raise the group size to
$K=8$, which the lecture variance argument predicts should reduce the fraction of degenerate
groups, give a better-conditioned $\sigma_r$, and lower the variance of $\hat A_i$ (effective
sample size grows with $K$). The second modification targets a \emph{reward-hacking} failure that becomes visible once $K=8$
trains stably. The baseline reward weights the format/shaping terms and the correctness term
roughly equally, and by step $\sim$3364 the $K=8$ policy formats almost perfectly while answering
only about half of the prompts correctly --- yet still earns $\approx 7/10$ reward. The policy is
therefore largely maximising the cheap, dense format terms rather than accuracy. To counter this I
resume that format-competent checkpoint and train a further $\sim$2.5k steps (5864 total) with a
reward reweighted toward answer accuracy, replacing the baseline's coarse step-function
correctness term with a denser clipped-exponential accuracy reward (a smooth, bounded function of
the answer error). \textbf{Predictions:} (i) raising $K$ to 8 stabilises training (sustained
reward, bounded KL, stable response length) and recovers or improves held-out accuracy over the
base model; (ii) the accuracy-weighted reward raises exact accuracy without harming format. Because
the two runs optimise different reward functions, the second prediction must be read from accuracy,
not from the (now lower) absolute reward.

\paragraph{Controlled-comparison design.}
\noindent
The comparison holds the GSM8K test split, greedy decoding, and seed-42 ordering fixed, and
evaluates the full 1319-example test set for the base model, the $K=2$ baseline, the matched
$K=8$ run, and the $K=8$ new-reward run. All three GRPO runs use the same data and schedule
(nominal 6516 steps, $\times 0.9 = 5864$ training steps). Uncertainty is a non-parametric
bootstrap over the 1319 prompts (10{,}000 resamples, seed 42), using the per-question eval
dumps in \path{tpu-2026-fork/analysis/}.

Paired full-test deltas (shared bootstrap resample indices over the 1319 prompts) sharpen the
pattern. $K=8$ beats the base model by $+8.64$ points, CI $[+5.84,+11.45]$; the new reward

result as suggestive rather than cleanly separated from the $K=8$ baseline. The robust claim is
that increasing $K$ rescues training from a catastrophic collapse and that the new reward
preserves, and perhaps slightly improves on, that gain.

\paragraph{Training dynamics.}
Figure~\ref{fig:reward-comparison} overlays the mean-reward curves. Because mean reward is only
comparable \emph{within a fixed reward function}, this figure shows the two default-reward runs;
the new-reward run is assessed through KL (Fig.~\ref{fig:kl-comparison}) and held-out accuracy.
Reward here is the mean per-rollout total reward $\bar r$ (summed over the four reward terms,
Tunix's \texttt{score/mean}; range $\approx[-4.5,10]$). The $K=8$ reward is sustained around
$7.5$ for most of training, whereas $K=2$ peaks early (around step 200) and then collapses by
step $\sim$1000, sitting at the $-2.5$ format-penalty floor with only brief thrashing recoveries. The KL curves (Fig.~\ref{fig:kl-comparison}) tell the same story:
$K=2$ produces a large KL excursion at the collapse ($\approx 10$ near step 1000) and stays
erratic, while both $K=8$ runs hold a low, bounded reference-KL of $\approx 0.2$--$0.3$.

\paragraph{Diagnostic: response-length collapse.}
Figure~\ref{fig:diagnostic} plots mean completion length, a known GRPO failure mode. The $K=2$
policy collapses to near-zero-length (degenerate, empty) completions after step $\sim$1000 ---
the same point at which reward and KL collapse --- punctuated by an unstable blow-up to
$\sim$600 tokens around steps 2500--3200 before collapsing again. Both $K=8$ runs instead hold a
stable $\sim$300--350 tokens for the whole run; the new reward trims length slightly (final
$\approx 251$ vs $\approx 305$ tokens) without hurting accuracy.

\paragraph{Findings.}
Raising $K$ from 2 to 8 turns a catastrophic failure ($0.08\%$, far \emph{below} the base
model's $47.38\%$) into a substantial improvement ($56.03\%$, $+8.64$ points over base). The
correctness-heavy reward adds a further suggestive $+2.50$ points to $58.53\%$, but its paired CI
includes zero, so I do not claim it as a separated effect. The reward-hacking signature is clear,
though: format accuracy is essentially unchanged across the two $K=8$ runs ($94.8\%\to94.3\%$)
while exact accuracy rises, exactly the pattern the reweighting was designed to produce --- it
shifted learning pressure onto correctness without sacrificing the already-solved formatting. The
diagnostic confirms the $K=2$ mechanism:
$K=2$ does not merely train with higher variance, it collapses into a degenerate empty-output
attractor, which the $K=8$ runs entirely avoid.

\paragraph{Theory connection.}
The collapse is exactly the within-group-variance pathology from the lectures. With $K=2$,
$\sigma_r$ is frequently zero or noisy, so the standardised advantage carries little signal and a
large amount of noise; the effective sample size per update is minimal and the policy is free to
drift, which the KL spike at the collapse makes visible. Increasing $K$ raises the chance of
mixed-outcome groups, conditions $\sigma_r$, and lowers advantage variance, keeping updates
inside the KL budget and the policy on-distribution (stable length, sustained reward). One
prediction was contradicted: I expected $K=2$ to train poorly but not to fall below the
\emph{base} model. The diagnosis is that degenerate-group dominance plus an unconstrained KL
excursion drove the policy into an empty-output mode that destroys format and hence accuracy ---
a failure of the advantage \emph{signal}, not of model capacity.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
Each setting is a single seed, and the new-reward gain over $K=8$ is not separated from zero. Both
long $K=8$ runs follow a deliberate two-phase curriculum: a 3364-step phase-1 base (default reward)
on which the policy learns to format, then a $\sim$2.5k-step phase~2 --- default reward for the
$K=8$ baseline, accuracy-weighted for the new-reward run. Their per-step metrics were logged only
to local TensorBoard and were reconstructed by stitching the shared phase-1 base to each run's
phase-2 events (the resume reset the reward/length step counter while KL kept the global step); the
curves are continuous across the boundary. A clean reward-specific claim would still need full
logging and a second seed.
