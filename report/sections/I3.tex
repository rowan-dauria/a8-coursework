\subsection{I.3 Improving on the Baseline}

\paragraph{Motivation and hypothesis.}
\textbf{Modification 1 (group size).} The baseline uses $K=2$ with the standardised advantage
$\hat A_i = (r_i - \bar r)/\sigma_r$ (see I.2). With two samples $\sigma_r$ is poorly estimated, and
a group is \emph{degenerate} whenever both completions earn equal reward ($\sigma_r=0$, advantage
ill-defined, no gradient); even non-degenerate groups give a tiny effective sample size. Raising
$K$ to $8$ should, by the lecture variance argument, reduce degenerate groups, condition $\sigma_r$,
and lower advantage variance.
\textbf{Modification 2 (reward).} Once $K=8$ trains stably it exposes a \emph{reward-hacking}
failure: format and correctness terms are weighted roughly equally, so by step $\sim$3364 the policy
formats almost perfectly yet answers only $\approx$half correctly while still earning $\approx7/10$
reward --- it is maximising the cheap, dense format terms. I resume that checkpoint and train a
further $\sim$2.5k steps (5864 total) with the reward reweighted toward accuracy, replacing the
coarse step-function correctness term with a denser clipped-exponential accuracy reward (smooth,
bounded in the answer error).
\textbf{Predictions:} (i) $K=8$ stabilises training (sustained reward, bounded KL, stable length)
and recovers or beats the base model; (ii) the accuracy-weighted reward raises exact accuracy
without harming format --- read from accuracy, not the (now lower) absolute reward.

\paragraph{Controlled-comparison design.}
\noindent
The comparison holds the GSM8K test split, greedy decoding, and seed-42 ordering fixed, and
evaluates the full 1319-example test set for the base model, the $K=2$ baseline, the matched
$K=8$ run, and the $K=8$ new-reward run. All three GRPO runs use the same data and schedule
(nominal 6516 steps, $\times 0.9 = 5864$ training steps). Uncertainty is a non-parametric
bootstrap over the 1319 prompts (10{,}000 resamples, seed 42), using the per-question eval
dumps in \path{analysis/}.

\paragraph{Findings and theory.}
Raising $K$ from 2 to 8 turns a catastrophic failure ($0.08\%$, far \emph{below} base $47.38\%$)
into a substantial gain ($56.03\%$, $+8.64$ over base); the correctness-heavy reward adds a
suggestive $+2.50$ to $58.53\%$, but its paired CI includes zero so I do not claim it as separated.
The reward-hacking signature is nonetheless clear: format accuracy is essentially unchanged across
the two $K=8$ runs ($94.8\%\to94.3\%$) while exact accuracy rises --- the pressure shift the
reweighting targeted. The $K=2$ collapse is the within-group-variance pathology from the lectures:
$\sigma_r$ is frequently zero or noisy, so the standardised advantage carries little signal, the
effective sample size is minimal, and the policy drifts (the KL spike). Raising $K$ creates
mixed-outcome groups, conditions $\sigma_r$, lowers advantage variance, and keeps updates inside the
KL budget. One prediction was contradicted --- I expected $K=2$ to train poorly, not to fall
\emph{below} the base model: degenerate-group dominance plus an unconstrained KL excursion drove it
into an empty-output attractor that destroys format and hence accuracy, a failure of the advantage
\emph{signal}, not of capacity.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
Each setting is a single seed and the new-reward gain is not separated from zero. Both long $K=8$
runs follow a two-phase curriculum (3364-step default-reward phase~1, then $\sim$2.5k-step
phase~2); per-step curves were reconstructed by stitching the shared phase~1 to each run's phase~2
events. A clean reward-specific claim needs full logging and a second seed.
