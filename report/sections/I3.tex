\subsection{I.3 Improving on the Baseline}

\paragraph{Motivation.}
\textbf{Modification 1 (group size).} The baseline uses $K=2$ with the standardised advantage
$\hat A_i = (r_i - \bar r)/\sigma_r$ (see I.2). With two samples $\sigma_r$ is poorly estimated, and
a group is \emph{degenerate} whenever both completions earn equal reward ($\sigma_r=0$, advantage
ill-defined, no gradient); even non-degenerate groups give a tiny effective sample size. Raising
$K$ above $2$ is the most direct theory-motivated fix: it should reduce degenerate groups, condition
$\sigma_r$, and lower advantage variance. A team sweep of $K\in\{2,4,8,16\}$
(Table~\ref{tab:ksweep}), alongside further single-knob parameter runs (Table~\ref{tab:other-runs}),
settled the operating point at $K=8$, which gave the clearest eval gain and the most stable training.
\textbf{Modification 2 (reward).} Once $K=8$ trains stably it exposes a \emph{reward-hacking}
failure: with format and correctness weighted roughly equally, the policy formats almost perfectly
while answering only $\approx$half correctly. We downweight the format terms and replace the coarse
step-function correctness term with a denser, accuracy-weighted clipped-exponential reward, then
resume the $K=8$ checkpoint for a further $\sim$2.5k steps (5864 total).

\paragraph{Controlled-comparison design.}
\noindent
The comparison holds the GSM8K test split, greedy decoding, and seed-42 ordering fixed, and
evaluates the full 1319-example test set for the base model, the $K=2$ baseline, the matched
$K=8$ run, and the $K=8$ new-reward run. All three GRPO runs use the same data and the same
optimisation schedule (nominal 6516 steps, $\times 0.9 = 5864$ training steps). We normalise by
training step rather than wall-clock or FLOPs: step-matching holds the number of gradient updates,
the learning-rate schedule, and the data ordering fixed, so any difference is attributable to the
per-step advantage estimate rather than to a longer or shorter optimisation run. The cost is that
$K=8$ spends roughly $4\times$ the rollout compute per step; under the one-week v6e-1 budget,
the training step was the controllable axis. The $K=2$ baseline is the I.1 configuration trained to
this same 5864-step schedule, and it collapses under the longer schedule (see training dynamics
below). Uncertainty is a non-parametric bootstrap over the 1319 prompts (10{,}000 resamples,
seed 42), using the per-question eval dumps in \path{evaluation/}. Cross-run differences use a
\emph{paired} bootstrap (the same resample indices applied to both models), with exact McNemar
tests Holm-corrected across the six pairwise comparisons.

\paragraph{Training dynamics and diagnostic.}
Figure~\ref{fig:dynamics} overlays the held-out curves (every 64 steps). Reward is comparable only
\emph{within} a fixed reward function, so the left panel shows the two default-reward runs and the
new-reward run is judged by KL and accuracy. The $K=8$ reward is sustained around $7.5$, whereas
$K=2$ peaks early (step $\sim$300) and collapses by step $\sim$1000 to the $-2.5$ format-penalty
floor. KL (right panel) tells the same story: $K=2$ spikes at the collapse (raw KL $\approx10$, 64-step
mean $\approx1$) and stays erratic, while both $K=8$ runs hold a bounded $\approx0.2$--$0.3$. The diagnostic
(Figure~\ref{fig:length-diagnostic}), mean eval completion length --- a known GRPO failure mode ---
pins the mechanism: at step $\sim$1000 $K=2$ collapses to near-empty completions, with an unstable
blow-up to $\sim$600 tokens over steps 2300--3100 before settling at a $\sim$40-token mode; both
$K=8$ runs hold a stable $\sim$300--360 tokens (new-reward $\approx362$ vs default $\approx352$).

\paragraph{Why response length, not advantage variance.}
The coursework suggests an advantage-variance diagnostic, but $\hat A_i=(r_i-\bar r)/\sigma_r$ is
standardised \emph{per group}, so its within-group variance is $\approx1$ by construction (Tunix sets
$\hat A_i=0$ when $\sigma_r=0$) and a $\mathrm{Var}(\hat A_i)$ curve is uninformative. The genuinely
informative quantity is the share of degenerate groups (the spread of $\sigma_r$ \emph{across} groups,
tied to $K_{\mathrm{eff}}$, I.4), but this was not logged, so we use mean completion length --- the
strongest GRPO-failure-mode signal the logged scalars support.

\paragraph{Findings and theory.}
Raising $K$ from 2 to 8 turns a catastrophic failure ($0.08\%$, far \emph{below} base $47.38\%$)
into a substantial gain ($56.03\%$, paired $+8.64$ over base, CI $[+5.84,+11.45]$); the
correctness-heavy reward adds a suggestive $+2.50$ to $58.53\%$ ($+11.14$ over base, CI
$[+8.42,+13.80]$), but the incremental gain over $K=8$ is not significant (paired CI $[-0.08,+5.00]$,
McNemar $p=0.06$ Holm-corrected) so we do not claim it as separated. The reward-hacking signature is
nonetheless clear: format accuracy is statistically flat across the two $K=8$ runs
($94.8\%\to94.3\%$, paired $-0.53\%$, CI $[-2.05,+0.99]$) while exact accuracy rises --- the
pressure shift the reweighting targeted. The $K=2$ collapse is the within-group-variance pathology from the lectures:
$\sigma_r$ is frequently zero or noisy, so the standardised advantage carries little signal, the
effective sample size is minimal, and the policy drifts (the KL spike). Raising $K$ creates
mixed-outcome groups, conditions $\sigma_r$, raises the effective sample size $K_{\mathrm{eff}}$
(lowering gradient-estimator variance), and keeps updates inside the KL budget. One prediction was contradicted --- we expected $K=2$ to train poorly, not to fall
\emph{below} the base model: degenerate-group dominance plus an unconstrained KL excursion drove it
into an empty-output attractor that destroys format and hence accuracy, a failure of the advantage
\emph{signal}, not of capacity.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
Each setting is a single seed. Both $K=8$
runs share phase~1 (3364 default-reward steps), so their per-step curves were stitched from this
shared phase~1 onto each run's $\sim$2.5k-step phase~2; a clean reward claim needs a second seed.
