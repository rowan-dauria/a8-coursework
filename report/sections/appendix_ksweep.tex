\subsection{Group-size ($K$) sweep}

Alongside the two flagship full-test runs in I.3, the team ran a quick sweep of the GRPO
group size $K \in \{2,4,8,16\}$ to locate a sensible operating point. These runs are
evaluated on a fast 64-example subset of the GSM8K test split (greedy decoding, seed 42),
\emph{not} the 1319-example bootstrap used in I.3, so the numbers are indicative rather than
the headline result; this is why the sweep lives in the appendix. The $K=2$, $4$ and $8$ runs
are matched at 3364 training steps; the $K=16$ run crashed at step 2991 and its evaluated
checkpoint is around 2500 steps, so its slightly lower score relative to $K=8$ is partly a
shorter-training artefact rather than purely a $K$ effect. Commits are GitHub commits (the
code was later ported to the GitLab repository, see the title page).

\noindent
The sweep supports the I.3 conclusion. Eval reward and all three accuracy metrics jump sharply
from $K=2$ to $K=4$ and then plateau: $K=8$ and $K=16$ track each other near the top of
Figure~\ref{fig:ksweep-eval-reward} ($\approx 7.5$), $K=4$ settles lower ($\approx 6.5$), and
$K=2$ climbs early before collapsing after step $\sim$500 to the format-penalty floor --- the
same degenerate-group, within-group-variance pathology diagnosed in I.3. (This 3364-step $K=2$
run, \texttt{jgs4c6kl}, is a different, shorter run from the 5864-step $K=2$ baseline
\texttt{keh7f5es} in I.3; both collapse, just at different points, so the $3.12\%$ here and the
$0.08\%$ there are consistent.) Beyond $K=8$ there is no clear gain --- $K=16$ matches $K=8$ on
the reward curve and is no better on accuracy despite the larger group --- while wall-clock time
grows monotonically with $K$ (more rollouts per step). $K=8$ is therefore the natural
sweet spot: it captures essentially all of the variance-reduction benefit at noticeably lower
compute than $K=16$, which is why the flagship I.3 runs use $K=8$.
