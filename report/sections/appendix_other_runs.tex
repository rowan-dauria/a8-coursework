the team ran several further single-knob experiments to probe the baseline: the learning rate,
the LoRA rank/$\alpha$, the reference-KL coefficient $\beta$, and an explicit length penalty
plus an empty-completion penalty. All use seed 42, the default 3364-step schedule, and the same
greedy 64-example GSM8K eval protocol as the rest of the report. For reference, the frozen base
model (\(\pi_{\text{ref}}\)) scores \textbf{47.38\%} on the full 1319-example test set.

\noindent
The dominant pattern echoes the $K$ sweep: the $K{=}8$ variants (LR 1e-5, rank-128 $K{=}8$,
length-penalty $K{=}8$, hard/medium data) hold a high, stable eval reward, whereas the $K{=}2$ variants collapse
or thrash --- most starkly $\beta{=}10^{-6}$, where removing the reference-KL penalty removes the
\emph{only} active trust region (the PPO clip never binds because the single inner iteration makes
the importance ratio $\equiv1$) and the policy drifts off-distribution. Where greedy accuracy
exists it tells a consistent story: neither a larger learning rate ($K{=}8$, $29.69\%$) nor extra
adapter capacity ($K{=}2$, $32.81\%$) beats the base model, and both inflate \emph{format}
adherence far above accuracy --- the same format-vs-correctness reward-hacking that motivated the
I.3 reward reweighting. Extra capacity alone does not rescue $K{=}2$, consistent with the collapse
being a variance / trust-region problem rather than a capacity one. The empty-completion penalty
lifts the collapsed $K{=}2$ baseline from $3.12\%$ to $14.06\%$ but stays far below base, slowing
the empty-output collapse without preventing it. These runs are exploratory and several are either
eval-pending or confounded, so they are recorded here rather than in the main I.3 comparison.
